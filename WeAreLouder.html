p5.js WEB APPLICATION

Protesting it’s more than a legal right, it’s an opportunity to shout out loud and express what has been difficult to say by other means.
Most of the protest are preceded by a general feeling of discontent, usually accumulated by years of governments that overstepped people’s rights.
It’s then a call out when people can not stand this kind of trait anymore Aand wants to be heard.

Protest has many forms, many symbols and many ways that people use in the attempt of cause a certain disruption in order to boost desired and needed changes on society.
Even though they mean to be peaceful, protests usually encounter many obstacles along the way. Many turn into violence, finding several confrontations with the public force or, very often, being stigmatised by means of communication.
 This situation is terrible for the people living in those countries.

But what about the people living abroad that want to show their support? They feel they cannot do much to help in a fight they believe in.
What if they can support their fights from all over the world without facing the obstacle of being far away in a physical way?  
Our website aims to be a place that allows people living abroad, to be part of the movement. We are convinced that people should have the right to be part of all the initiatives happening in the world. We want to give them a voice, to make them be heard, to let them be louder. 

 How? We were inspired by “cacerolazo”:  
Cacerolazo comes from the Spanish word “cacerola” that means pot. One of the main elements people is using to show their discomfort is the sound of banged pots. The cacerolazo consists of a group of people making noise by banging pots, pans and other kitchen utensils in order to call for attention.
  This sound is going to be the main element, the symbol used to join these movements in a pacific way.

On the application you can find out about the protests currently active in every part of the world and show your support by filling out a geolocation form.
This way you will hear the sound of your cacerola reaching that of other protestants around the world, displayed as pulsating dots on a world-map.
